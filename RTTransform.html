The Geometry of Light

The Transform Tab (indicated by the set square icon) is where you define the physical boundaries and structural integrity of your image. While the Exposure and Color tabs deal with light and pigment, the Transform tab deals with pixels, perspective, and optics.

1. Crop Tool

The Crop tool allows you to remove distracting edges and redefine your composition. Unlike many editors, RawTherapee allows you to see the "cropped-out" pixels by default, which can be toggled in the top toolbar.

  • Aspect Ratio: Lock to original, custom, or standard print sizes.
  • Guides: Use Rule of Thirds, Golden Ratio, or Harmony to aid composition.
Clean Visualization: If you find the dotted crop box distracting, go to Preferences > Image Processing and set "Crop editing guides shown when not editing" to None. Also, use the black/gray/white icons in the top toolbar to "black out" the area you're cutting away for a cleaner preview.

2. Resize

This is crucial for web export. If you want your final image to be smaller than the raw sensor data, you must enable this module.

  • Scale: Resize by percentage or fixed pixel dimensions.
  • Post-Resize Sharpening: Essential for maintaining crispness after downsampling.
The Secret to Sharp Exports: Never rely on global sharpening alone for web images. Downscaling inherently softens pixels. Always enable Post-Resize Sharpening within the Resize module to re-introduce definition to the new, smaller pixel grid.

3. Lens Geometry & Profile Correction

Most modern lenses have inherent distortion (barrel or pincushion). This module fixes that automatically using LCP profiles or manually via sliders.

Vignetting Correction

Removes the dark corners caused by lens optics. You can use the "Automatic" setting if an LCP profile is loaded, or adjust the strength and midpoint manually.

4. Perspective Control

Crucial for architectural and street photography. Use this to straighten "leaning" buildings or correct a tilted horizon.

  • Vertical/Horizontal: Correct keystoning.
  • Auto-Crop: Automatically zooms the image to fill the frame after a perspective shift so you don't see empty "black wedges."
Keep it Natural: When correcting vertical perspective (e.g., tall buildings), try not to go to a perfect 90-degree parallel. Leaving a slight 2-3% "lean" often looks more natural to the human eye than a mathematically perfect correction.

5. Rotation & Auto-Fill

The most precise way to level a horizon is the "Select Straight Line" button. Click it, drag a line along your horizon, and RawTherapee does the rest.

Pro Workflow: The Golden Rule

Transform First, Polish Later: Always perform your Crops, Rotations, and Lens Corrections before fine-tuning your Sharpening or Noise Reduction. Large geometrical shifts can slightly alter the appearance of fine noise and detail; seeing the final "shape" of your image first ensures you aren't sharpening pixels you plan to cut away later.
